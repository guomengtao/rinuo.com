What is Obsidian?

Obsidian is a powerful note-taking application that allows you to create a personal knowledge base using Markdown files stored locally on your device.

The Origin Story

Obsidian was created by Shida Li and Erica Xu, founders of Dynalist Inc. The application first appeared in public beta in March 2020 and has since grown into a popular tool among knowledge workers, researchers, and students.

Name Meaning

The name "Obsidian" comes from the natural volcanic glass known for its smooth surface that can be polished to create a mirror. This reflects the app's purpose as a tool for reflecting and connecting your thoughts.

Core Philosophy

Obsidian follows a local-first approach, meaning your data remains on your device by default. It leverages the power of bidirectional links to help you build a network of thoughts, similar to how the human brain works.

Powerful Features

Obsidian offers a rich set of features to help you organize your thoughts and build a comprehensive knowledge base.

Bidirectional Links

Create connections between notes with [[wikilinks]] and visualize your knowledge graph.

Markdown Support

Write in Markdown with support for equations blocks, diagrams, callouts, and more.

Local Storage

Your notes are stored locally as plain text files, giving you complete control over your data.

Plugin Ecosystem

Extend functionality with hundreds of community plugins and themes.

Sync Options

Sync your notes across devices using Obsidian Sync or third-party services like Dropbox.

Privacy Focused

Your data remains private by default, with end-to-end encryption for sync.

Pros & Cons

An honest look at what makes Obsidian shine and where it could improve.

Advantages

  • Local-first approach gives you complete control over your data
  • Powerful bidirectional linking creates a network of thoughts
  • Extensive customization through themes and plugins
  • Works offline with no dependency on internet connectivity
  • Free core functionality with optional paid features
  • Strong community support and regular updates

Disadvantages

  • Steeper learning curve compared to simpler note-taking apps
  • Some advanced features require a paid subscription
  • Mobile experience is not as polished as desktop
  • Can feel overwhelming for new users
  • No real-time collaboration in the free version
  • Syncing across devices requires extra setup or subscription
