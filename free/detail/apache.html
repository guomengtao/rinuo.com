About Apache

The world's largest and most influential open-source software foundation

A Brief History

The Apache Software Foundation (ASF) was founded in 1999, born from the Apache HTTP Server project that began in 1995. The name "Apache" came from the respect the original developers had for the Native American Apache tribe, known for their endurance and skill in warfare.

What started as a small group of developers collaborating on a web server has grown into a global community of over 8,500 contributors across six continents, governing more than 350 open-source projects.

The ASF operates under a meritocratic governance model, where decisions are made by those who contribute, ensuring the foundation remains focused on technical excellence and community collaboration.

Founding Visionaries

The pioneers who started the Apache revolution

Brian Behlendorf

Co-founder & First Chairman

A key figure in the early days of the web, Behlendorf was instrumental in organizing the Apache Group and establishing the foundation's collaborative culture. He later became the CTO of the World Economic Forum.

Roy Fielding

Co-founder & HTTP Expert

A principal author of the HTTP specification and creator of REST architectural style. Fielding's work laid the groundwork for how the web functions today through his research on networked systems.

Doug Cutting

Creator of Lucene & Hadoop

While not an original founder, Cutting's contributions were pivotal. He created Lucene, Nutch, and Hadoop - technologies that became cornerstones of Apache's big data ecosystem.

Advantages & Considerations

Evaluating Apache software for your projects

Key Advantages

  • Free and open-source with permissive licensing
  • Enterprise-grade reliability and security
  • Massive, active community support
  • Scalable solutions for projects of all sizes
  • Interoperable with most modern technologies
  • Proven track record with decades of refinement

Considerations

  • Steeper learning curve for complex projects
  • Configuration complexity for optimal performance
  • May be overkill for small, simple applications
  • Documentation can be technical and dense
  • Rapid release cycles require ongoing maintenance
  • Some projects have overlapping functionality
